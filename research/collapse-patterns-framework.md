## 1. Introduction and Methodology

### 1.1 Purpose

This document provides an empirical foundation for Monte Carlo simulation of geopolitical collapse scenarios. It synthesizes historical case studies into quantified parameters, probability distributions, and causal pathways that can be used to generate probabilistic future scenarios for states and regions facing similar risk profiles.

### 1.2 Definitions

**Civilizational Collapse** (for purposes of this framework): A severe, sustained deterioration in the functioning of a state or region characterized by one or more of:
- GDP decline ≥40% from peak
- Excess mortality ≥2% of population
- Population displacement ≥15% of population
- Sustained hyperinflation (≥50% monthly)
- State loss of territorial control ≥25%
- Healthcare/infrastructure system failure affecting majority of population

**Partial Collapse**: Meeting 1-2 of the above criteria  
**Severe Collapse**: Meeting 3-4 of the above criteria  
**Total Collapse**: Meeting 5+ of the above criteria or complete state failure

### 1.3 Methodological Notes

**Data Quality Warnings:**
- Statistics from collapsed states are inherently unreliable
- Estimates often vary by factors of 2-5x (especially mortality figures)
- Where ranges exist, both bounds are provided
- Probability estimates are informed judgments, not statistical calculations
- All parameters should be treated as distributions, not point estimates

**Analytical Framework:**
- Historical cases analyzed for: preconditions, triggers, progression, effects, recovery
- Cross-case pattern analysis identifies common pathways and parameters
- Regional assessments apply historical patterns to current conditions
- Monte Carlo parameters derived from historical distributions with expert adjustment

## 3. Collapse Pathway Taxonomy

### 3.1 Primary Collapse Pathways

Based on historical cases, six primary collapse pathways are identified:

#### Pathway A: Economic/Financial Collapse

**Exemplar cases:** Venezuela, Lebanon, Zimbabwe  
**Mechanism:** Currency/banking system failure → hyperinflation → economic paralysis

**Typical sequence:**
1. Structural imbalances accumulate (deficits, debt, dependency)
2. Confidence shock (external or internal)
3. Capital flight / bank run
4. Currency collapse
5. Hyperinflation
6. Economic activity collapse
7. Infrastructure decay
8. Healthcare/service collapse
9. Mass emigration (if exit available)

**Typical timeline:** 2-5 years from trigger to acute phase

**Key parameters:**
| Variable | Distribution | Notes |
|----------|--------------|-------|
| GDP decline | 50-80% | Mean ~65% |
| Peak inflation | 1,000%-1B%+ monthly | Log-normal |
| Emigration | 15-30% of population | If exit available |
| Duration of acute phase | 3-7 years | |

#### Pathway B: Political Fragmentation / Civil War

**Exemplar cases:** Syria, Yugoslavia, Libya  
**Mechanism:** Political breakdown → armed conflict → societal destruction

**Typical sequence:**
1. Political legitimacy crisis
2. Protest/opposition emergence
3. State repression
4. Armed resistance
5. Civil war
6. Territorial fragmentation
7. External intervention
8. Protracted conflict OR resolution

**Typical timeline:** 6-24 months from protest to civil war

**Key parameters:**
| Variable | Distribution | Notes |
|----------|--------------|-------|
| Death toll | 1-5% of population | Highly variable |
| Displacement | 30-60% of population | Very high |
| GDP decline | 50-80% | Infrastructure destruction |
| Duration | 5-15+ years | Resolution uncertain |

#### Pathway C: Resource/Food System Failure

**Exemplar cases:** North Korea, historical famines  
**Mechanism:** Agricultural/distribution system failure → famine

**Typical sequence:**
1. Agricultural system vulnerability
2. External shock (weather, subsidy loss, war)
3. Production collapse
4. Distribution system failure
5. Famine conditions
6. Mass mortality OR emigration
7. Chronic food insecurity (if regime survives)

**Typical timeline:** 1-3 years from shock to famine

**Key parameters:**
| Variable | Distribution | Notes |
|----------|--------------|-------|
| Excess mortality | 2-15% of population | If exits blocked |
| Child malnutrition | 30-60% stunting | Long-term effects |
| Duration of acute phase | 2-4 years | |
| Probability of regime survival | 50-80% | Counter-intuitively high |

#### Pathway D: Institutional Decay

**Exemplar cases:** Venezuela (pre-hyperinflation), many developing states  
**Mechanism:** Gradual hollowing of state capacity → eventual crisis

**Typical sequence:**
1. Institutional capture/corruption
2. Service delivery degradation
3. Brain drain begins
4. Infrastructure maintenance deferred
5. Trigger event exposes fragility
6. Rapid collapse of degraded systems
7. Transition to other pathway (A, B, or C)

**Typical timeline:** 10-20 years of decay, then rapid collapse

**Key parameters:**
| Variable | Distribution | Notes |
|----------|--------------|-------|
| Decay period | 10-20 years | Slow process |
| Warning indicators | Visible but ignored | Normalcy bias |
| Collapse speed (once triggered) | 1-3 years | Faster than expected |

#### Pathway E: External Shock Amplification

**Exemplar cases:** Lebanon (port explosion), various natural disasters  
**Mechanism:** External shock overwhelms weakened system

**Typical sequence:**
1. Pre-existing vulnerabilities
2. Major external shock (disaster, war, pandemic)
3. System overwhelmed
4. Cascading failures
5. Transition to other pathway

**Key insight:** External shocks don't cause collapse in resilient systems. They expose and amplify existing fragility.

#### Pathway F: Demographic Collapse (Slow-Motion)

**Exemplar cases:** Russia (1990s mortality), potential future cases  
**Mechanism:** Sustained below-replacement fertility + elevated mortality

**Typical sequence:**
1. Fertility decline below replacement
2. Population aging
3. Working-age population decline
4. Fiscal pressure (pensions, healthcare)
5. Service delivery decline
6. Further fertility decline (feedback loop)
7. Either: immigration offset OR sustained decline

**Typical timeline:** 30-50 years (generational)

**Key parameters:**
| Variable | Distribution | Notes |
|----------|--------------|-------|
| Fertility rate (crisis level) | <1.3 | South Korea: 0.7 |
| Population decline rate | 0.5-2% annually | Compounds |
| Working-age decline | 1-3% annually | Fiscal impact |

### 3.2 Pathway Interactions and Compound Collapse

Most severe collapses involve multiple pathways interacting:

| Case | Primary Pathway | Secondary Pathways |
|------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Venezuela | A (Economic) | D (Institutional decay precondition) |
| Syria | B (Civil war) | A (Economic), C (Food) |
| Zimbabwe | A (Economic) | D (Institutional), C (Food stress) |
| North Korea | C (Food) | D (Institutional rigidity) |
| Russia 1990s | D→A (Institutional→Economic) | F (Demographic) |

**Compound collapse probability:** When two or more pathways interact, severity increases non-linearly. Historical cases suggest:
- Single pathway: 40-60% GDP decline typical
- Two pathways: 60-80% GDP decline typical
- Three+ pathways: 70-90% GDP decline, potential state failure

## 6. Contagion and Spillover Models

### 6.1 Refugee/Migration Spillover

**Model:** Refugees from collapsing state X create pressure on neighboring states Y₁, Y₂, etc.

**Distribution formula:**
```
Refugees_to_Y = Total_Refugees × (Border_Length_Y / Total_Border_Length) × 
                Accessibility_Factor_Y × Pull_Factor_Y
```

**Typical distributions:**
| Neighbor Type | Share of Refugees | Capacity Impact |
|---------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Primary neighbor (land border, similar culture) | 40-60% | High |
| Secondary neighbor (land border, different culture) | 15-30% | Moderate |
| Regional destination (non-adjacent) | 10-20% | Low-Moderate |
| Distant destination (developed countries) | 5-15% | Low |

**Historical examples:**
- Syria → Turkey: ~3.6 million (60% of external refugees)
- Syria → Lebanon: ~1.5 million (25% of Lebanese population)
- Venezuela → Colombia: ~2.5 million
- Zimbabwe → South Africa: ~2-3 million

**Destabilization threshold:** When refugees exceed ~5-10% of host population, significant political and economic stress emerges.

### 6.2 Economic Contagion

**Trade channel:**
- Collapse of major trading partner reduces exports
- Impact proportional to trade concentration
- Formula: GDP_Impact_Y = Export_Share_to_X × GDP_Decline_X × Elasticity

**Financial channel:**
- Banking sector exposure to collapsing country
- Sovereign debt holdings
- FDI exposure

**Remittance channel:**
- Collapse reduces remittance outflows to origin countries
- Can trigger secondary crises in remittance-dependent states

### 6.3 Security Spillover

**Conflict diffusion:**
- Armed groups cross borders
- Weapons proliferation
- Ethnic/sectarian mobilization spreads

**Historical diffusion patterns:**
| Source Conflict | Spillover Conflicts |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| Syrian civil war | Iraqi instability, Lebanese tensions, Turkey-PKK |
| Libyan collapse | Sahel destabilization, Mali, Niger |
| Afghan civil wars | Pakistan instability, Central Asian tensions |

### 6.4 Cascading Collapse Probabilities

**Conditional probabilities (if neighbor X collapses):**

| Neighbor Y Characteristics | P(Y collapse | X collapse) |
|---------------------------|------------------------------|
| Already high-risk, shares ethnic groups | 30-50% |
| Moderate-risk, significant refugee inflow | 15-25% |
| Moderate-risk, limited exposure | 5-15% |
| Low-risk, strong institutions | 2-8% |
