# The 21st Century in Demographic and Climatic Terms

## A Strategic Assessment of National and Regional Trajectories

---

## Executive Summary

The 21st century will be shaped by the interaction of two slow-moving but inexorable forces: demographic transition and climate change. Neither operates in isolation. Aging societies must fund adaptation while their tax bases shrink. Climate stress displaces populations into regions already grappling with migration politics. Agricultural disruption threatens food security precisely as working-age populations decline in the regions that produce surplus food.

This assessment proceeds from several baseline assumptions derived from observable trends rather than optimistic projections:

**On emissions**: Global emissions are unlikely to decline rapidly enough to avoid severe climate impacts. Powerful interests in hydrocarbon-producing states and consuming economies have demonstrated sustained capacity to delay meaningful action. Planning should assume 2.5-3.5°C warming by 2100, with attendant disruptions to agricultural systems, water availability, and human habitability in vulnerable regions.

**On political response**: Western democracies will largely turn away from equatorial crisis. The political incentives favor border enforcement over humanitarian intervention. Geographic barriers will be allowed to function as passive filters, with deaths occurring at a distance from wealthy-nation publics. This is not a prediction of what should happen, but of what observable trends suggest will happen.

**On demographic trajectories**: Below-replacement fertility is now near-universal outside Sub-Saharan Africa. This is a structural condition unlikely to reverse through policy intervention. Immigration remains the only mechanism for rapid demographic adjustment, but political constraints on immigration are tightening rather than loosening across the developed world.

The result is a century of divergence: some regions will adapt and maintain roughly stable conditions despite challenges, while others face compounding crises that may fundamentally alter their demographic trajectories through elevated mortality, collapsed fertility, or mass displacement.

---

## Tiered Assessment Overview

Countries and regions can be roughly grouped by their expected capacity to navigate combined demographic and climate pressures:

**Tier 1 — Best Positioned**: Geographic advantages, institutional capacity, demographic challenges manageable through immigration if politically feasible. The United States, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand fall into this category—not because they face no challenges, but because their structural advantages exceed those of any alternative.

**Tier 2 — Significant Challenges, Likely Manageable**: Meaningful climate exposure or demographic pressure, but sufficient resources and institutional capacity for adaptation. Outcomes depend heavily on policy choices and external factors. Northern Europe (contingent on AMOC stability), Japan, South Korea, and Southern South America occupy this tier, though with very different risk profiles.

**Tier 3 — Severe Stress**: Major climate impacts, demographic imbalances, or institutional weaknesses that will require fundamental adaptation. Outcomes range from managed decline to crisis depending on choices made. Southern Europe, China, Russia, and much of South and Southeast Asia fall here.

**Tier 4 — Potential Catastrophe**: Combinations of climate exposure, demographic pressure, and limited adaptive capacity that may produce humanitarian catastrophe including large-scale excess mortality. The Sahel, Horn of Africa, parts of the Middle East, low-lying coastal regions, and some small island states face existential risks.

These tiers are not predictions of outcomes but assessments of structural position. Policy choices, technological developments, and unpredictable events will alter trajectories. But structural position constrains the range of plausible outcomes.

## Regional Assessments

### North America

#### United States

The United States enters the 21st century with structural advantages that no other major power can match: vast territory spanning multiple climate zones, abundant agricultural capacity with room for northward migration of growing zones, relatively favorable demographics compared to peer nations, strong institutions despite evident stress, unmatched military capacity, and geographic distance from the most climate-stressed regions.

These advantages do not guarantee good outcomes. American political dysfunction is severe and possibly worsening. The country's one clear demographic advantage—historical openness to immigration—has become intensely contested, and policy may foreclose this option precisely when it matters most. Regional disparities are large and growing: the Southwest faces existential water constraints, while the Upper Midwest and Pacific Northwest may become more attractive. Internal climate migration will create political economy disruptions as shrinking states retain disproportionate political power.

Fertility sits around 1.6-1.7, below replacement but higher than most wealthy nations. Without immigration, the US would face gradual population decline; with continued immigration at historical levels, modest growth remains possible. The foreign-born population will likely increase as a share of the total, with attendant political tensions.

The honest assessment: the United States is likely to remain the world's dominant power and one of the most livable wealthy nations through the 21st century, but will fail to capitalize fully on its advantages due to political paralysis. It will manage its own climate adaptation reasonably well while doing little to address global climate stress, and will enforce its borders with increasing vigor as pressure mounts. Living standards may stagnate or decline for portions of the population even as aggregate metrics remain strong.

#### Canada

Canada's position is in many respects even stronger than that of the United States: vast territory, abundant fresh water, enormous potential for agricultural expansion as warming extends growing seasons northward, low population density, and stable institutions with less evident political dysfunction than its southern neighbor.

The constraints are real but manageable. Much of Canada's territory is currently unsuitable for agriculture due to thin soils and short growing seasons; climate change improves the temperature constraint but not necessarily the soil constraint. Infrastructure in northern regions is minimal. Permafrost degradation will damage existing northern infrastructure before new development becomes viable.

Canada's immigration policy is more explicitly points-based and economically selective than American policy, and enjoys broader political support. This positions Canada well for continued demographic management through immigration, though it raises questions about the fate of less-skilled climate migrants who may seek entry.

Canada's primary risks are second-order: economic integration with the United States means American dysfunction affects Canadian outcomes; climate refugees unable to reach Canada will accumulate to the south, potentially destabilizing the US-Canada relationship; and truly large-scale global disruption would not spare even well-positioned northern nations.

#### Mexico

Mexico occupies an intermediate position: not as severely exposed as Central America, but facing real climate stress, particularly in the north where water scarcity is already acute. The country has undergone demographic transition more rapidly than often recognized; fertility has fallen to near-replacement levels and the population will likely peak and begin declining later this century.

Mexico's future depends heavily on its relationship with the United States. It will experience continued pressure as a transit country for Central American migrants. Its northern border regions face water stress that climate change will intensify. The industrial economy, particularly manufacturing integrated with US supply chains, provides economic resilience but also vulnerability to American policy shifts.

Mexico is likely to remain a middle-income country facing significant challenges but avoiding catastrophe—neither a refuge nor a disaster zone, but a buffer state absorbing pressure from the south while maintaining integration with the north.

### Europe

Europe's trajectory may diverge more sharply from current expectations than any other wealthy region, primarily due to two factors: the uncertain fate of the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC), and migration pressure from climate-stressed regions to the south.

#### The AMOC Question

The AMOC—the system of Atlantic currents that transports warm water northward, giving Western Europe its anomalously mild climate—shows signs of weakening and may be approaching a tipping point. Scientific assessment remains uncertain, with some models suggesting collapse could occur mid-century while others indicate greater resilience. Recent research suggests the risk has been systematically underestimated in major climate models.

If the AMOC collapses or severely weakens, the consequences for Europe would be transformative. Average temperatures in northwestern Europe could fall by 5-10°C or more over decades, potentially more than offsetting greenhouse warming. Precipitation patterns would shift dramatically, with severe drought intensification across southern Europe. Agricultural capacity would be devastated. The Europe that emerges would be a fundamentally different place—colder, drier, and far less productive than the continent that built modern industrial civilization.

The probability of this outcome remains genuinely uncertain—perhaps 10-40% this century depending on emissions trajectories and model assumptions. This uncertainty itself is a planning problem: Europe cannot know whether to prepare for a warming or cooling future.

#### Northern Europe (Assuming AMOC Stability)

If the AMOC remains functional, Northern Europe—Scandinavia, the UK, Germany, the Low Countries—faces climate challenges but from a position of relative strength. Direct physical impacts are moderate compared to other regions. Institutional capacity is high. Economic resources for adaptation are substantial.

The binding constraint is migration. Europe sits immediately north of regions facing some of the most severe climate impacts on Earth: North Africa, the Sahel, the Middle East. These regions contain hundreds of millions of people facing increasingly unlivable conditions with limited exit options. The migration pressure this generates will dwarf the 2015 refugee crisis that nearly fractured the European Union.

European politics has already demonstrated limited capacity to manage migration at current levels. The rise of nationalist parties across the continent reflects genuine popular resistance to large-scale immigration. The EU's inability to agree on burden-sharing mechanisms suggests this will not improve.

The likely trajectory: Northern European countries will invest heavily in border enforcement, accept modest managed migration while turning back unauthorized arrivals, and allow deaths in the Mediterranean and Sahara to function as deterrents. Living standards will remain high for existing populations but the humanitarian cost of this stance will be substantial. Political fragmentation between immigration-restrictive and immigration-tolerant factions will intensify.

Demographic pressures are significant, with fertility rates around 1.5-1.7 across the region. Unlike the United States or Australia, European countries have struggled to integrate large immigrant populations, and political tolerance for the immigration levels that would offset demographic decline does not exist. Gradual population decline and aging are the most likely trajectory.

#### Southern Europe

The Mediterranean basin is a climate change hotspot. Southern Europe—Spain, Portugal, Italy, Greece—already experiences severe heat, water scarcity, and wildfire risk that will intensify substantially. Agricultural productivity will decline. Tourism, a major economic sector, may shift northward as summers become dangerously hot.

These countries enter this stress period with weak fiscal positions, high debt loads, low fertility (among the world's lowest), and young populations already emigrating northward within Europe. The combination of direct climate stress and demographic hollowing creates a compounding spiral: fewer workers to fund adaptation, reduced economic dynamism, further emigration.

Southern Europe is also on the front line of Mediterranean migration. Italy and Spain have borne disproportionate burdens in previous migration waves and will face intensifying pressure. Political systems in these countries have already shown stress, with the rise of hard-right parties in Italy and Spain.

The trajectory is concerning: economic stagnation or decline, continued demographic hollowing, increasing climate stress, and political instability. These countries will remain part of the European Union and the developed world, but their position within that world will deteriorate significantly. Some recovery of previously abandoned rural areas by climate migrants from elsewhere in Europe is possible, but integration challenges will be severe.

#### Eastern Europe

The post-communist states of Eastern Europe face a distinct set of challenges. Climate exposure is moderate—less severe than Southern Europe, with some potential benefits from warming in northern areas. But demographic collapse is already advanced. Countries like Bulgaria, Romania, and the Baltic states have experienced massive emigration since EU accession, draining working-age populations to Western Europe.

These countries now have among the world's lowest fertility rates and fastest-shrinking populations. Unlike Western Europe, they lack the wealth to attract compensating immigration and the institutional capacity to manage it effectively. They are also among the most politically resistant to non-European immigration.

The trajectory is managed decline: shrinking populations, aging workforces, reduced economic significance, but without catastrophic outcomes. Living standards will likely remain above global averages but the relative position of these countries will deteriorate. Some may essentially empty out, retaining aging populations in cities while rural areas depopulate entirely.

### Russia

Russia is among the most difficult cases to assess, combining potential climate benefits with severe demographic decline, institutional decay, and the overhang of the Ukraine conflict.

The theoretical upside: Russia possesses vast territory, including Siberian regions that could become more agriculturally productive as temperatures rise. Warming may open Arctic shipping routes and access to previously frozen resources. Russia has substantial fossil fuel reserves that will retain value during any extended transition period.

The severe constraints: Russian institutions have decayed substantially under authoritarian consolidation. State capacity to execute large-scale development of new agricultural or resource frontiers is questionable. Demographic decline is severe, with a fertility rate around 1.5 and population already falling. The working-age population is shrinking rapidly, and the Ukraine war has accelerated this through casualties, emigration of skilled workers, and foregone births.

Infrastructure built on permafrost faces degradation as warming continues. Pipelines, railways, buildings, and roads in Siberia are already experiencing damage. The cost of maintaining or replacing this infrastructure is enormous.

The honest assessment: Russia's climate position is better than often assumed but its institutional position is worse. The country may theoretically benefit from warming but likely lacks the state capacity and human capital to realize those benefits. Continued decline in relative power and living standards is probable, with potential for more severe crisis depending on political trajectories. Russia remains a nuclear power and thus a factor in global security regardless of its internal condition.

### Central Asia

The five Central Asian republics—Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, Tajikistan, and Kyrgyzstan—face a distinct and severe climate challenge: water scarcity driven by glacial retreat.

The major river systems of the region (Amu Darya, Syr Darya) depend on glacial meltwater from the Tian Shan and Pamir mountain ranges. These glaciers are retreating. The pattern will be temporary abundance as accumulated ice melts, followed by permanent scarcity as the glaciers disappear. The Aral Sea disaster—one of the 20th century's greatest environmental catastrophes—demonstrates how this can unfold.

Agriculture in the region, particularly Uzbekistan's cotton sector, is water-intensive and already strains available resources. Population growth continues in the southern countries (Tajikistan, Uzbekistan), compounding pressure on declining water supplies.

These countries are caught between Russian and Chinese spheres of influence, with authoritarian governments of variable competence. Kazakhstan possesses hydrocarbon wealth and somewhat stronger institutions; the others are poor, with Tajikistan among the poorest countries in Asia.

The trajectory is severe stress with high probability of localized catastrophe. Water conflicts between upstream and downstream countries are possible. Large-scale displacement from water-scarce agricultural regions is likely. This is a region that will export instability to its neighbors—including Russia, China, and Afghanistan—rather than one that can manage its challenges in isolation.

### East Asia

#### China

China faces what may be the most severe combination of demographic and climate pressures of any major power, compressed into a shorter timeframe than comparable challenges elsewhere.

The demographic situation is critical. Fertility has collapsed to roughly 1.0, among the lowest ever recorded. The population began declining in 2022-2023. The working-age population is already shrinking and will decline by hundreds of millions by century's end. Unlike earlier demographic transitions in wealthy countries, China is aging before it has become rich—reaching these conditions at roughly $12,000-13,000 per capita GDP rather than the $40,000+ levels of Japan or Germany. The accumulated wealth and institutional buffers that allowed other countries to manage demographic transition do not exist at the same level.

The legacy of the one-child policy compounds this: the 4-2-1 problem, where single adults may be responsible for two parents and four grandparents. Sibling support networks that cushion aging elsewhere do not exist. Social safety nets are underdeveloped by wealthy-nation standards.

Climate exposure is severe. Water scarcity in northern China, where much of the population and agricultural production is concentrated, is already critical and will worsen. The North China Plain faces potentially lethal wet-bulb temperatures with increasing frequency. Heat mortality will skew heavily toward the elderly population that will constitute an ever-larger share of the whole. Agricultural stress adds pressure on food security for a country that feeds 20% of global population on 7% of arable land.

China lacks the immigration option that theoretically allows other countries to offset demographic decline. Han nationalism and the political system's emphasis on ethnic homogeneity make large-scale immigration culturally and politically inconceivable. Automation and productivity gains are the only available responses, and these have limits in care work and services.

The political dimension is critical. The Chinese Communist Party's legitimacy rests substantially on delivering economic growth and rising living standards. Sustained stagnation or decline tests that social contract in ways the system has never experienced. Whether the political system can adapt to managing decline rather than growth is unknown.

The trajectory: China will likely experience significant relative decline over the 21st century, potentially losing its position as the world's largest economy to the United States or India depending on how each nation's challenges unfold. This need not mean catastrophe—Japan has managed demographic decline without political collapse—but China faces the challenge with less wealth per capita and more severe climate exposure. The range of outcomes is wide, from managed decline to systemic crisis.

#### Japan

Japan presents the most mature example of demographic decline among major economies. Fertility has been below replacement for decades; the population has been declining since 2010. The population pyramid is inverted, with the elderly constituting an ever-larger share.

Yet Japan has managed this transition without political instability or dramatic decline in living standards. Per capita GDP remains high. Institutions remain functional. Social order is maintained. This offers a partial model for other aging societies.

The critical difference for China: Japan reached this condition at very high levels of accumulated wealth and institutional development. Japan's adaptation has also involved technological investment (robotics, automation) and maintenance of export competitiveness, and has been cushioned by relatively stable geopolitical circumstances.

Japan's 21st century trajectory is continued gradual decline in population and relative economic weight, with maintenance of high living standards for a shrinking population. Climate exposure is moderate—heat, typhoon intensification, some sea level rise—but manageable given Japan's resources. The country will become less globally significant but will remain wealthy and stable.

#### South Korea

South Korea presents the extreme case of demographic collapse: fertility around 0.7-0.8, the lowest in the world. At sustained rates this low, the population would decline by half within a generation. No society has ever navigated this condition.

Whether current fertility represents a temporary trough or a new equilibrium is unknown. South Korean society combines extreme educational and economic pressure on young people, very high housing costs, demanding work culture, and traditional gender expectations that have not adapted to women's workforce participation. The result is that many young Koreans simply do not have children.

The government has invested heavily in pro-natalist policy with minimal effect. Immigration remains politically and culturally difficult.

South Korea's climate exposure is moderate. Its primary challenge is purely demographic, and it is more severe than any country has previously faced. The range of outcomes is wide—from adaptation through some combination of technological change and eventual fertility recovery, to severe economic and social disruption. South Korea is essentially running an uncontrolled experiment in extreme low fertility.

#### Taiwan

Taiwan faces demographic pressures similar to South Korea—very low fertility, aging population—combined with the obvious geopolitical risk from Chinese territorial claims. Climate exposure is moderate (typhoon intensification, some heat stress).

Taiwan's 21st century trajectory depends critically on whether it remains functionally independent or is absorbed by China. The demographic challenge is severe but in principle manageable for a wealthy, technologically advanced society. The geopolitical risk is existential.

### South and Southeast Asia

#### India

India's trajectory is among the most consequential for global outcomes given its population (now the world's largest) and its combination of significant challenges with significant potential.

The demographic situation is less dire than China's. Fertility has fallen to near-replacement (around 2.0) but the population will continue growing until mid-century due to demographic momentum, potentially reaching 1.7 billion before stabilizing and declining. The age structure remains relatively young. Unlike China, India has not yet lost its demographic window.

Climate exposure is severe. Large portions of the country face dangerous heat with increasing frequency. Water stress is acute in many regions, with groundwater depletion critical. The monsoon is becoming more erratic, with implications for agricultural productivity. Sea level rise threatens coastal cities including Mumbai and Kolkata. Hundreds of millions of Indians live in climate-vulnerable conditions.

Institutional capacity is mixed. India has maintained democratic governance and experienced significant economic growth, but state capacity varies enormously by region and function. The ability to execute large-scale adaptation infrastructure is uncertain.

The trajectory is highly variable depending on economic development, governance evolution, and the severity of climate impacts. India has the potential to become a major global power—the demographic and economic fundamentals support this—but also faces risks of severe climate disruption that could overwhelm adaptive capacity. More than most countries, India's outcome remains genuinely open.

#### Pakistan

Pakistan combines severe climate exposure with institutional weakness and political instability in a way that makes it one of the higher-risk major countries.

Climate impacts are already severe: the 2022 floods displaced millions and caused enormous damage. Glacial melt in the Himalayas and Karakoram threatens the Indus River system on which Pakistani agriculture depends. Heat exposure is extreme, with parts of the country regularly experiencing dangerous temperatures.

The population continues growing despite falling fertility, creating pressure on already-strained resources. Institutions are weak, with a history of military intervention in politics and chronic governance failures. The economy is fragile, dependent on external support.

The trajectory is concerning: Pakistan faces mounting climate stress with limited capacity for adaptation and a political system that has failed to address even current challenges. Large-scale displacement, food insecurity, and potential state fragility are plausible outcomes. As a nuclear-armed state, Pakistani instability has implications beyond its borders.

#### Bangladesh

Bangladesh is the canonical example of climate vulnerability: a low-lying deltaic nation with extremely high population density, located in the path of intensifying cyclones and facing severe sea level rise.

Projections suggest large portions of the country could become uninhabitable due to inundation, salination of agricultural land, and increased storm damage. The population at risk numbers in the tens of millions. The only comparable historical analog would be the potential flooding of the Netherlands, but the Netherlands has the resources for massive engineering responses and options for evacuation; Bangladesh has neither.

Where displaced Bangladeshis would go is an open question. India has already built border barriers and shown little willingness to accept large-scale Bangladeshi immigration. The physical barriers to migration are not as severe as the Sahara, but the political barriers may be nearly as effective.

Bangladesh has shown significant adaptive capacity—disaster response has improved substantially, and the country has achieved notable development gains despite its challenges. But the scale of potential displacement may exceed any realistic adaptive capacity.

#### Southeast Asia

The nations of Southeast Asia face varied but generally severe climate exposure, with somewhat better institutional capacity than South Asia.

Indonesia, the world's fourth-largest country, is an archipelagic nation facing sea level rise, heat intensification, and stress on its tropical agriculture. The country has undergone demographic transition (fertility near replacement) and has achieved middle-income status. Its trajectory depends on continued economic development and adaptation investment.

Vietnam faces similar challenges with somewhat stronger state capacity. Thailand, an older and wealthier nation, faces moderate climate stress with better resources for adaptation. The Philippines, with continued population growth and extreme typhoon exposure, is more vulnerable.

The Mekong Delta region—spanning Vietnam, Cambodia, and Laos—faces particular risk from changing river flows as upstream damming in China and glacial retreat alter the hydrological system.

Generally, Southeast Asia faces severe climate exposure but is not in the catastrophic category of South Asia or Africa. These countries have demonstrated adaptive capacity and have resources for continued adaptation, though they will be significantly stressed.

### Middle East and North Africa

#### Gulf States

The oil-producing Gulf states—Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait—face a distinctive situation: extreme climate exposure combined with enormous financial resources.

These countries are already pushing the limits of human habitability during summer months, with wet-bulb temperatures approaching dangerous thresholds. They survive through universal air conditioning, desalination, and imported food—all purchased with hydrocarbon revenues.

The medium-term challenge is the energy transition. If global oil demand declines substantially, the revenue base that makes current living arrangements possible disappears. These countries are aware of this risk and investing in economic diversification with varying success.

The long-term challenge is physical: continued warming may make outdoor activity impossible during summer months, and even indoor survival may become dependent on continuous air conditioning that requires reliable electricity. A power grid failure during a severe heat event could be catastrophic.

The population is largely non-citizen, with guest workers constituting the majority in most Gulf states. A contraction of revenues would likely mean mass expulsion of foreign workers, creating a migration crisis in source countries (South Asia, Southeast Asia, Africa).

The trajectory depends on the pace of energy transition and these countries' success at diversification. Slow transition gives them time; rapid transition could create crisis.

#### Egypt

Egypt combines population pressure, water scarcity, and political authoritarianism in a volatile mix. The country's 100+ million people depend on the Nile, which is increasingly contested by upstream nations (particularly Ethiopia's Grand Renaissance Dam) and threatened by climate-altered flows.

Agricultural land is limited to the Nile Valley and Delta. Sea level rise threatens the densely populated Delta region. The economy is weak and dependent on external support.

Egypt has historically been a source of regional stability through its size and military capacity. A destabilized Egypt would transform the Eastern Mediterranean and North African region. Large-scale Egyptian displacement would add substantially to Mediterranean migration pressure.

#### The Levant and Iraq

Syria's civil war provided a preview of how climate stress (the drought that preceded the war) can interact with political fragility to produce catastrophe. Syria remains devastated; reconstruction prospects are limited.

Iraq combines water scarcity (the Tigris and Euphrates are stressed by upstream damming in Turkey and reduced flows), heat exposure, and post-conflict institutional weakness. Parts of Iraq may become uninhabitable due to heat within decades.

Jordan, Lebanon, and the Palestinian territories face water scarcity and dependence on increasingly unstable neighbors. Israel has demonstrated advanced adaptive capacity (desalination, efficient irrigation) but its political situation introduces risks that purely climate-economic analysis cannot capture.

#### The Maghreb

Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, and Libya face intensifying heat and water scarcity across the Atlas region and northern Sahara margin. These countries sit between European destinations and sub-Saharan source regions for migration.

Their role as transit countries—and their willingness to control migration flows in exchange for European support—is a critical variable in Mediterranean migration dynamics. State fragility in Libya has already demonstrated how breakdown in this buffer zone affects European migration pressure.

### Sub-Saharan Africa

Sub-Saharan Africa presents the most severe humanitarian risks of the 21st century and the greatest uncertainties in standard demographic projections.

#### The Standard Projection and Its Uncertainties

UN median projections show Sub-Saharan Africa's population growing from roughly 1.2 billion today to potentially 3-4 billion by 2100. This would represent nearly 40% of humanity. The projection assumes a gradual fertility transition similar to that experienced by other regions as development proceeds.

Climate change introduces fundamental uncertainty into these projections. Large portions of the region face conditions that may become incompatible with current population levels—not through gradual stress but through potential uninhabitability. The standard demographic projections do not adequately integrate climate constraints.

#### The Sahel

The Sahel—the semi-arid band stretching from Senegal to Sudan—represents the most severe convergence of climate stress, demographic pressure, and limited adaptive capacity.

The region is already experiencing desertification, erratic rainfall, and agricultural failure. Lake Chad has shrunk by approximately 90% since the 1960s. Armed conflict driven partly by resource competition has destabilized multiple countries. State capacity is minimal throughout the region.

Population continues growing despite these stresses, with fertility rates among the world's highest. This is the demographic pattern that would shift most under climate pressure—not through successful development and voluntary fertility decline, but through increased mortality and crisis-driven fertility reduction.

The critical geographic factor: exit from the Sahel requires crossing the Sahara. The desert is already one of the deadliest migration corridors on Earth. As temperatures rise, the crossing becomes more dangerous and potentially impossible without vehicular support. The populations facing the worst conditions may be physically unable to reach potential refuge.

This produces the trapped population phenomenon: people who cannot sustain themselves where they are but cannot physically reach anywhere better. The historical precedents for this situation are uniformly catastrophic—famine, conflict, and excess mortality measured in millions.

The trajectory, absent major intervention: elevated mortality, reduced fertility through crisis rather than development, ongoing conflict and state failure, and humanitarian catastrophe largely invisible to wealthy-nation publics because it occurs beyond physical reach. Deaths will be counted in millions to tens of millions over the century.

#### West Africa (Coastal)

Coastal West Africa—Nigeria, Ghana, Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire—faces serious climate challenges (heat, sea level rise, agricultural stress) but is not in the trapped population situation of the Sahel. Migration routes to North Africa and Europe, while dangerous, remain traversable.

Nigeria is particularly consequential given its population (over 200 million, projected to exceed 400 million). It is currently larger than any European country and may become larger than the United States. Nigerian outcomes therefore matter at global scale.

The trajectory for coastal West Africa is severe stress with varied outcomes depending on governance quality and economic development. Some countries may achieve middle-income status and build adaptive capacity; others may face state failure and mass displacement.

#### The Horn of Africa

Ethiopia, Somalia, Eritrea, and Djibouti face severe drought, food insecurity, and conflict. Ethiopia's civil conflicts and external tensions add political instability to climate stress. Somalia has experienced state failure for decades with limited recovery.

This region is a consistent source of refugees and will likely remain so. Migration routes to Europe via Libya or Egypt are established but dangerous. Migration to the Gulf states provides an alternative outlet.

#### Central and Southern Africa

Central Africa (Democratic Republic of Congo, Central African Republic, etc.) faces different challenges: less severe heat stress due to tropical forest cover, but deforestation pressure, conflict, and institutional weakness. The DRC contains vast natural resources but has failed to translate these into development.

Southern Africa faces water scarcity that will intensify. South Africa, the region's most developed economy, already experiences periodic water crises and electricity shortages. Zimbabwe and Zambia depend on agriculture and hydropower vulnerable to changing rainfall patterns.

Southern Africa is better positioned than the Sahel or Horn—more resources, better infrastructure, functioning states in some countries—but will experience significant stress and may see reversed development gains.

### Latin America

#### Central America

Central America's "dry corridor" (Guatemala, Honduras, El Salvador, Nicaragua) is already experiencing agricultural disruption from changed rainfall patterns. Coffee production—a major economic sector—is moving upslope as optimal growing zones shift. Subsistence agriculture is failing.

This region is the primary source of migration pressure on the US southern border. Population sizes are modest (Northern Triangle combined is roughly 35 million), so the absolute numbers are manageable from a US perspective, but the human impact on source communities is severe.

The trajectory is continued climate stress, continued emigration pressure, and likely decline in remaining populations as young workers leave. These countries may partially empty out, with populations concentrated in urban areas and diaspora communities rather than rural agriculture.

#### Caribbean

Caribbean island nations face existential risk from sea level rise and hurricane intensification. Many are small enough that a single severe storm can devastate the entire nation (as demonstrated by Hurricane Maria in Dominica and Puerto Rico).

The larger Caribbean nations (Cuba, Dominican Republic, Jamaica) face less existential risk but significant climate stress. Migration to the United States is well-established for many Caribbean populations.

The trajectory for small island states may be functional abandonment over the century—not immediate displacement but gradual emigration as repeated storm damage and sea level rise make rebuilding untenable.

#### Northern South America

Venezuela has already experienced civilizational collapse largely independent of climate—the product of political mismanagement—displacing over 7 million people to neighboring countries. Recovery prospects are limited.

Colombia, Ecuador, and Peru face varied climate exposure: Andean glacial retreat threatens water supplies for major cities; Amazon regions face deforestation and potential ecosystem transition; coastal areas face sea level rise. These countries have functioning states and economic activity that provides some adaptive capacity.

Brazil is a continental-scale case with enormous variation. The Amazon basin faces potential transition from forest to savanna under continued warming and deforestation—a tipping point that would have global climate implications. The populous southeast faces water stress. The northeast faces intensifying drought. The south is relatively better positioned.

Brazil's political trajectory will significantly influence outcomes. Recent governments have oscillated between environmental protection and development-focused deforestation. The Amazon's fate is a global concern, but governance is Brazilian.

#### Southern South America

Argentina, Chile, Uruguay, and southern Brazil constitute one of the potential climate refugia of the 21st century. The Southern Cone's distance from the equator provides thermal protection; water resources, while stressed in some areas, are generally better than comparable latitudes elsewhere; population density is low with room for absorption.

The constraints are institutional rather than physical. Argentina's chronic economic instability and political dysfunction undermine adaptive capacity. Chile faces severe water stress in its central regions, including Santiago, despite better overall governance. These countries struggle to provide for current populations.

The theoretical potential: the Southern Cone could become a destination for climate migrants from northern South America and potentially beyond, developing agricultural capacity in warming Patagonia and absorbing displaced populations into its empty spaces.

The likely reality: institutional weakness prevents full realization of this potential. The region will experience relatively less severe climate impacts than most of the world but will not become a major refuge. Modest improvement in relative position is likely, without dramatic transformation.

### Oceania

#### Australia

Australia combines genuine climate resilience with genuine climate vulnerability in ways that require careful parsing.

The vulnerabilities are real: Australia is already the driest inhabited continent, experiencing severe drought, catastrophic bushfires, and water stress. The 2019-2020 fires demonstrated climate change as present reality. Much of the interior is uninhabitable, and the habitable coastal fringe faces its own risks.

The resilience is also real: Australia has built significant desalination capacity, with major plants serving all major cities. Perth already derives a substantial portion of its water from desalination. Expansion is underway. Australia has the technical and financial capacity to ensure water security for its coastal populations through desalination—expensive, but feasible.

Australia's demographic situation mirrors other wealthy nations: fertility around 1.5 and falling, below replacement, with population growth dependent on immigration. Unlike Europe, Australia has maintained relatively strong political support for managed immigration, though not for unauthorized arrivals.

Australia's border enforcement is among the world's most aggressive: offshore detention, boat turnbacks, and policies designed to deter maritime arrivals. This "fortress Australia" approach has been politically successful domestically and will likely intensify as regional climate pressure mounts.

Southeast Asia and the Pacific Islands will experience severe climate stress, generating migration pressure on Australia. Australia's geographic isolation—thousands of kilometers of ocean—makes unauthorized arrival difficult. Australia will likely maintain its selective approach: accepting skilled migrants through managed programs while preventing unauthorized arrivals by sea.

The trajectory: Australia will remain wealthy, stable, and among the world's more desirable places to live through the 21st century. It will achieve this partly through investment in adaptation (desalination, fire management, agricultural technology) and partly through aggressive exclusion of climate refugees. The moral weight of that exclusion will fall on others.

#### New Zealand

New Zealand shares Australia's geographic isolation with somewhat better climate positioning—more temperate, better water availability, less severe heat and fire exposure. Population is small (5 million) with similar fertility and immigration patterns to Australia.

New Zealand's brand as a "clean, green" refuge has attracted attention, including tech billionaires building bolt-holes. Whether New Zealand actually accepts significant climate migration is uncertain—the country is small enough that even modest inflows change its character.

The trajectory is similar to Australia's: wealthy, stable, relatively insulated from the worst climate impacts, with the moral question of how much responsibility it takes for less fortunate neighbors.

#### Pacific Islands

Pacific Island nations face existential risk from sea level rise. Countries like Tuvalu, Kiribati, and the Marshall Islands may become largely or entirely uninhabitable within the century. Populations are small in absolute terms (hundreds of thousands combined), but the complete loss of national territory would be unprecedented in modern history.

Where these populations go is unresolved. Some have negotiated migration agreements with Australia and New Zealand; others have not. Climate migrants from drowned nations will test international frameworks that assume states continue to exist.

## Systemic Risks

Beyond regional assessments, several systemic risks could alter the trajectory for multiple regions simultaneously.

### Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation Collapse

As discussed in the European section, AMOC collapse would transform Northwestern Europe's climate, potentially more than offsetting greenhouse warming with circulation-driven cooling. This risk is specific to the North Atlantic region but severe enough to constitute an existential uncertainty for European planning.

Current scientific assessment suggests 10-40% probability of significant weakening or collapse this century, with tipping point potentially within the next few decades. The uncertainty itself is a planning problem—Europe cannot know whether its future is warming or cooling.

### Food System Disruption

Global food production is more geographically concentrated and internationally traded than at any point in history. A relatively small number of breadbaskets—the American Midwest, Brazilian Cerrado, Ukrainian/Russian steppes, Australian wheat belt, Indian and Chinese rice production—feed the world.

Climate impacts on any of these regions ripple through global markets. Simultaneous impacts on multiple breadbaskets—plausible given that many climate impacts (heat waves, droughts) are correlated across regions—could produce global food crisis.

Countries without domestic food production capacity are particularly vulnerable. The Middle East and North Africa import the majority of their grain; Sub-Saharan Africa is a net food importer despite having vast agricultural potential. Food price spikes have historically triggered political instability, including contributing to the Arab Spring.

### Cascading State Failure

State failure tends to spread. Syrian collapse destabilized Lebanon and contributed to the European migration crisis. Venezuelan collapse has strained Colombia, Peru, and Ecuador. Sahel instability has spread across borders.

Climate stress will likely produce state failures. The question is whether they remain contained or cascade. A world of multiple simultaneous state failures—particularly in nuclear-armed states like Pakistan—poses risks that regional analysis does not capture.

### Migration as a System

Migration will be a defining feature of the 21st century. The question is whether it occurs through managed channels or chaotic displacement.

The analysis throughout this document suggests that wealthy nations will attempt to manage migration through restriction rather than accommodation—border enforcement, selective admission of skilled migrants, deterrence of unauthorized arrivals. The human cost of this approach falls on those unable to migrate through authorized channels.

The physical constraints on migration—the Sahara, the Mediterranean, the Pacific and Atlantic oceans—function as filters even without policy enforcement. The people facing the worst conditions may be unable to reach potential refuge regardless of policy.

## Critical Uncertainties

Several factors could alter these trajectories significantly in either direction.

### Technology

The assessments throughout this document assume current technology and plausible near-term development. Breakthrough technologies could change the picture:

- Dramatically cheaper desalination could ease water constraints
- Agricultural technologies (drought-resistant crops, vertical farming) could increase production in stressed regions
- Geoengineering could theoretically reduce warming, though with enormous governance challenges and uncertain side effects
- Energy breakthroughs (fusion, advanced storage) could accelerate decarbonization

None of these can be relied upon, but none can be ruled out.

### Political Discontinuities

Political systems can shift more rapidly than demographic or climate trends. The analysis assumes roughly current political trajectories, but possibilities include:

- A major wealthy nation (US, Europe) shifting toward accepting large-scale climate migration
- Coordinated international response to climate displacement beyond current frameworks
- Conversely, more aggressive exclusion policies including militarized borders or preemptive intervention in source regions

### Emissions Trajectory

The analysis assumes continued high emissions consistent with current political economy constraints. More rapid decarbonization—whether through policy, technology, or economic shift—would reduce the severity of climate impacts, particularly in the second half of the century.

Conversely, feedback effects (permafrost methane release, Amazon dieback) could accelerate warming beyond current projections.

### Black Swans

Wars, pandemics, economic crises, and other discontinuities will occur. Their timing and nature cannot be predicted but will interact with demographic and climate trends. A major war involving China, for example, would dramatically alter East Asian trajectories regardless of underlying demographic trends.

## Conclusion

The 21st century will be defined by the interaction of demographic transition and climate change—two slow-moving forces that are already locked in and will unfold regardless of near-term policy choices.

The broad picture is one of divergence: a wealthy global minority in climate-resilient regions will adapt and maintain something like current living standards, while a global majority faces varying degrees of stress, disruption, and in the worst cases, catastrophe. The borders between these worlds will be enforced with increasing determination as the century progresses.

This is not a prediction of what should happen but an assessment of what observable trends suggest will happen. The moral weight of these outcomes is substantial. The political feasibility of alternatives is limited.

The most honest conclusion is uncertainty about specifics combined with confidence in the broad direction. Exact population figures, precise temperature changes, and specific political outcomes cannot be predicted. But the structural forces are in motion, the trajectories are largely set, and the range of plausible outcomes—while wide—does not include a return to 20th century assumptions about perpetual growth, development convergence, and expanding prosperity.

The 21st century will be harder than the 20th. How much harder, and for whom, depends on choices not yet made—but the range of those choices is narrower than we might wish.
