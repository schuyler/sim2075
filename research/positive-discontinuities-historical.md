> [!note] Framework Reframing
> This research was conducted under the "positive vs. negative discontinuities" framing. The [[methodology/integrated-event-state-framework]] has since moved away from valence-based classification. 
> 
> **What remains valid:**
> - Historical examples and their mechanisms
> - Pattern analysis of what enables major positive transitions
> - Candidate events for catalog inclusion
> 
> **What has changed:**
> - Events are now classified by **causal type** (Type 1/2/3), not valence
> - Impacts are specified as **vectors with durability types**, not "positive" or "negative"
> - The asymmetry question is reframed: different impact types have different durability (deaths are permanent; agreements require maintenance)
> 
> **How to use this research:**
> - Treaty/agreement events → Type 3 (Contingent) with maintenance_required durability
> - Technology adoption → Often NOT events (S-curves belong in baseline); breakthroughs may be Type 1
> - Political transitions → Type 2 (threshold) or Type 3 (contingent) depending on structure
> - The "candidate positive events" section provides starting points for event specifications

# Historical Positive Discontinuities (1975-2025)

## Overview

This note documents historical examples of positive discontinuities—events that materially improved trajectories beyond baseline expectations. The current event catalog has been criticized for focusing exclusively on negative discontinuities, which biases the model toward pessimistic outcomes and fails to capture the full distribution of possibilities.

Historical analysis reveals that positive surprises occur with meaningful frequency and can be as consequential as negative shocks.

## Political and Geopolitical

### Cold War Resolution (1989-1991)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Event** | Fall of Berlin Wall, peaceful Soviet dissolution, end of Cold War |
| **Timing** | 1989-1991 |
| **Why discontinuous** | Few analysts predicted timing or peaceful nature; removed existential nuclear war risk; opened Eastern Europe to integration |
| **Counterfactual** | Baseline expectation was continued standoff or violent transition |
| **Mechanism** | Leadership decisions (Gorbachev), economic exhaustion, popular mobilization, elite defection |

## Patterns and Mechanisms

### What Enables Positive Discontinuities?

1. **Leadership and agency** - Gorbachev, Mandela, Deng; individual decisions can unlock positive paths

2. **Crisis as catalyst** - India 1991, Latin American stabilization; crisis can overcome reform resistance

3. **Technology S-curves** - Once adoption begins, network effects and learning curves accelerate beyond linear projections

4. **Coordination when stakes are clear** - Montreal Protocol, post-2008; coordination problems can be solved when consequences are visible and attributable

5. **Demonstration effects** - Eastern European transitions, Asian development models; success in one place changes expectations elsewhere

6. **War exhaustion** - Northern Ireland, Cold War end; protracted conflicts can reach points where settlement becomes possible

7. **Institutional preparation** - EU framework enabled German reunification; prior institution-building can enable rapid positive transitions

### What Distinguishes Positive from Negative Discontinuities?

| Dimension | Negative | Positive |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Visibility** | Often dramatic, visible, sudden | Often gradual accumulation, less salient |
| **Attribution** | Easy to attribute to specific cause | Often diffuse, multiple contributing factors |
| **Media coverage** | Extensive | Under-covered relative to impact |
| **Expert prediction** | Often anticipated (though timing uncertain) | Often underestimated or dismissed |
| **Reversibility** | Many are irreversible (collapse, death) | Many are reversible (progress can be lost) |

### Implications for Modeling

1. **Asymmetric attention** - We naturally focus on downside risks; positive possibilities require deliberate attention

2. **Technology forecasting** - Historical pattern suggests systematic underestimation of technology adoption rates and cost declines

3. **Political transitions** - Expert consensus on "inevitable" conflict or instability has been wrong repeatedly

4. **Coordination** - International cooperation has succeeded in specific domains; not all collective action problems are intractable

5. **Tail distributions** - Both left and right tails should be fat; extreme positive outcomes are plausible
