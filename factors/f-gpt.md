# F_GPT: Great Power Tension Factor

## Overview

The Great Power Tension factor represents the intensity of strategic competition between major powers—primarily US, China, and Russia. When F_GPT has a high realization, conflicts, trade wars, and bloc formation events become more likely.

## What This Factor Captures

- US-China strategic competition intensity
- NATO-Russia tension levels
- Trade war and decoupling dynamics
- Military posturing and exercises
- Alliance cohesion and stress
- Sanctions escalation
- Nuclear stability

## Events That Load Heavily on F_GPT

| Event | Loading | Rationale |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| Taiwan Conflict | 0.85 | Primary US-China flashpoint |
| US-China Trade War Escalation | 0.80 | Direct great power economic conflict |
| NATO-Russia Conflict | 0.75 | Direct great power military risk |
| Korean Peninsula Crisis | 0.60 | US alliance commitment tested |
| Dollar Reserve Crisis | 0.50 | Financial dimension of competition |
| India-Pakistan Conflict | 0.30 | Great powers involved peripherally |

## Correlation Implications

High F_GPT realizations produce clusters of:
- Multiple regional flashpoints activating simultaneously
- Trade and financial fragmentation events
- Alliance stress events
- Sanctions escalations

Great power tension creates correlated risk across otherwise-separate theaters (Taiwan, Korea, Ukraine, Middle East).

## Relationship to Other Factors

- **F_EAS**: Strong relationship. East Asian tension is heavily driven by great power competition.
- **F_EUR**: Moderate relationship via NATO-Russia dynamics.
- **F_FIN**: Moderate relationship. Sanctions and decoupling affect financial system.
- **F_CLIM**: Weak relationship. Great power dynamics largely independent of climate.

## Calibration Notes

Great power tension is currently elevated (US-China competition, Ukraine conflict). The baseline probability of tension-related events reflects this starting condition.
